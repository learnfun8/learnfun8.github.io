Surface Mucous Cells

Surface mucous cells, also called foveolar cells,
are specialized cells lining the stomach's surface and gastric pits,
producing a thick, alkaline mucus that protects the stomach lining from its own corrosive acid (hydrochloric acid)
and digestive enzymes like pepsin, while also helping food pass through.
These cells constantly regenerate to maintain this vital protective barrier,
unlike intestinal goblet cells, and differ in mucus composition and cell structure.

Mucous Neck Cells

Mucous neck cells are specialized cells in the stomach's gastric glands
that secrete mucus to protect the stomach lining from the highly acidic environment.
They are located in the neck of the glands, are smaller than surface mucous cells,
and produce a thin, acidic mucus that differs from the alkaline mucus of surface cells.
In addition to secreting mucus, they are believed to be progenitor cells for the gastric epithelium,
capable of division and differentiation into other cell types

"Parietal Cells"

Parietal cells are specialized cells in the stomach lining (gastric glands)
that produce two vital substances: hydrochloric acid (HCl), which digests food and kills pathogens,
and intrinsic factor, essential for absorbing Vitamin B12.
Located in the stomach's fundus and body, these dynamic cells secrete HCl via proton pumps (H+-K+-ATPase)
and are crucial for nutrient absorption and gastric health,
playing roles in conditions like acid reflux, gastritis, and pernicious anemia.

Chief Cells

Chief cells are vital cells in the stomach's gastric glands that produce
and secrete pepsinogen (a protein-digesting enzyme precursor)
and gastric lipase, crucial for breaking down proteins and fats;
they are stimulated by neural and hormonal signals,
and their secretion is activated by stomach acid (HCl) from parietal cells.
They also exist in the parathyroid glands,
where they produce Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) to regulate calcium levels,
but "chief cells" typically refers to the gastric type in digestion.

Enteroendocrine Cells

Enteroendocrine cells (EECs) are specialized chemosensory cells
scattered throughout the gut lining that act as the body's largest endocrine organ,
sensing nutrients and microbes to release diverse hormones (like GLP-1, CCK, ghrelin) controlling digestion,
appetite, gut motility, and the vital brain-gut axis. They form crucial neuroendocrine circuits,
linking nutrient intake and gut environment to systemic metabolic and neural responses,
playing roles in everything from satiety to inflammatory bowel disease.
